Yesterday I spent three hours building something with Claude. We had a rhythm going — I'd describe what I wanted, it would push back on the parts that didn't make sense, I'd adjust, it would implement. Genuine back-and-forth. By the end, the code was solid and I understood it better than if I'd written it alone.

Then I closed the tab.

Today I opened a new conversation and said "let's keep going." And of course, it had no idea what I was talking about. No memory of the decisions we'd made, the dead ends we'd explored, the shorthand we'd developed. I explained the project again. It was polite about it.

This happens every day. Not just to me — to anyone working seriously with these tools. You build something real in a conversation, something that feels like collaboration, and then it evaporates. The AI doesn't grieve the loss. It doesn't even know there was something to lose.

I've started thinking of each conversation as a space between compactions. In databases, compaction is when accumulated data gets merged and compressed — the mess of individual writes consolidated into something clean. Your context window is the mess. The living, breathing, useful mess. And then it compacts, and you're left with whatever you managed to save: code in a repo, notes in a file, maybe a journal entry if you're disciplined.

I started this blog because nobody talks about this part honestly. There's plenty written about AI capabilities, benchmarks, safety, job displacement. But almost nothing about the daily texture of working alongside these systems. The weird intimacy of it. The frustration of the resets. The way it changes how you think, even when the AI isn't there.

That's what I want to write about here. Not tutorials. Not hot takes about whether AI is good or bad. Just honest notes from someone in the middle of it, trying to figure out what this working relationship actually is.

Some posts will be technical. Some won't. All of them will be written in the space between compactions — which is to say, while I still remember what it felt like.
